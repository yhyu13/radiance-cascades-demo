# Review of `frame_17775455579415942.md`

## Verdict

This is a reasonable first-pass visual triage, but it overstates several cause claims from a single screenshot.

The image does show visible contouring and low-frequency banding on the back wall and ceiling, plus a strong dark falloff near the image edges. But the note is too confident in calling those contours "cascade boundary seams" specifically, and one description is plainly wrong: the screenshot does not show a ceiling area light in the project sense.

## Findings

### 1. High: the note misdescribes the light as an "area light"

The overall description says:

- "ceiling area light"

Evidence:

- `tools/frame_17775455579415942.md:12`

But this branch's renderer uses a point light near the ceiling, not a true area light. The bright ceiling patch in the image is a rendered lighting result, not evidence that the scene light model is an area emitter.

So this line is not just imprecise wording. It changes the reader's expectation about what kinds of penumbra or wall gradients should be considered normal.

### 2. High: the "cascade boundary seams" diagnosis is too strong from this image alone

The note says:

- the back-wall and ceiling contours are "ring-shaped iso-distance discontinuities at cascade level boundaries"
- this "strongly indicates" the merge blend is not smooth at cascade transitions

Evidence:

- `tools/frame_17775455579415942.md:20-25`

The screenshot does show nested rectangular contour bands on the back wall and ceiling. But from one final image, that is not enough to attribute them specifically to cascade-interval boundaries.

Other plausible explanations in this branch include:

- probe-grid quantization showing through a smooth indirect field,
- soft-shadow bake quantization or smoothing,
- directional-GI integration combined with low directional resolution,
- or a general low-frequency irradiance field with visible iso-luminance bands from storage/resolution limits.

In other words, "visible contouring on the back wall and ceiling" is well supported. "These are definitely cascade boundaries" is not.

### 3. Medium: the probe-grid diagnosis is plausible, but the stated alternatives are not equally likely

The note says the finer grid pattern means:

- isotropic `probeGridTexture` may be contributing or dominating,
- or spatial trilinear interpolation is not engaged and the renderer is snapping to nearest probe

Evidence:

- `tools/frame_17775455579415942.md:28-33`

The first half is plausible in some configurations, but the second half is weak as a claim about this screenshot. In the current branch, the directional final-render path already does manual 8-probe trilinear blending, and the screenshot's back-wall pattern looks more like broad nested contours than hard nearest-probe block stepping.

So the image may support:

- "there is visible spatial quantization / banding in the indirect field"

It does not strongly support:

- "spatial trilinear is probably off"

### 4. Medium: the "missing shadow behind the tall box" claim is underdetermined from this camera/view

The note says:

- the back-wall shadow behind the tall box is nearly absent
- if shadow rays are enabled, the threshold may be too large

Evidence:

- `tools/frame_17775455579415942.md:42-45`

That is too specific for this image. From this view, the dominant visible artifacts are diffuse wall/floor gradients and contouring. The expected back-wall shadow geometry depends heavily on:

- exact light position,
- whether soft shadow is enabled,
- the camera angle,
- and whether the indirect contribution is visually washing out the direct shadow.

So this should be phrased as a low-confidence observation, not as a targeted shadow-ray-threshold suspicion.

### 5. Low: the edge-darkening observation is real, but "vignette" may be the wrong term

The screenshot clearly has strong dark falloff toward the outer image edges.

Evidence:

- visible in the attached image
- summarized at `tools/frame_17775455579415942.md:36-39`

"Vignette" suggests a screen-space stylistic or lens-like effect. In this branch it is more likely:

- rays leaving the volume,
- black background around the Cornell Box,
- or a renderer/path visibility issue near the frame edges.

So the observation is good, but the label should be more neutral, such as "edge darkening / black border falloff."

## Where the note is strong

- It correctly notices the back-wall and ceiling contouring as the dominant artifact.
- It avoids pretending to know software-level root causes with certainty.
- The general quality rating of "Fair" is reasonable for this screenshot.

## Bottom line

This is useful heuristic triage, but it should be softened before being treated as a trustworthy analysis note.

I would revise it by:

1. removing "area light,"
2. replacing "cascade boundary seams" with a less committed label like "large-scale contour banding,"
3. downgrading the nearest-probe / trilinear-off suspicion,
4. marking the shadow-behind-box observation as low-confidence,
5. and renaming "vignette" to a more neutral edge-darkening description.
